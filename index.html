The Many Definitions of Culture

One word, 160+ meanings

Culture is the human-made part of the environment. It can describe a nation, a region, a city, or a single business, and it shapes business, marketing, and consumption alike.

“The ideas, values, practices, and material objects that allow a group of people, even an entire society, to carry out their collective lives in relative order and harmony.” Ritzer, 2015 — cited in Baack & Baack Ch. 2

Selected definitions (Table 2.1)

  • The arts and manifestations of human intellectual achievement
  • The general customs and beliefs of a group at a particular time
  • Hofstede: “the collective programming of the mind” that distinguishes one group from another
  • Neuliep: the framework in which humans organise thoughts, emotions, and behaviour

Culture splits into material and subjective (symbolic) culture — with no clean line between them.

Material Culture

The physical things people make and use

Material culture = clothing, food, houses, tools, machines, art, buildings. Physical objects define members' behaviours and perceptions.

  • Clothing protects (light cotton in India, warm layers in northern Canada) and signals status. Many Poles iron even casual clothes; many Americans value comfort.
  • Technology is vital material culture in developed economies — students must master computers to function.
  • Sweets are material culture — the KitKat connection starts here.

Why it matters to marketers

The study of material culture asks how consumers relate to, use, and value objects. Product design, packaging, and the whole mix must fit those physical realities.

Japanese furniture, for instance, tends to be austere, functional, and low to the ground — an aesthetic that flows straight into product design choices.

Material Culture · Dietary Preferences

Food is culture you can taste

Cultural dictates shape when people eat, what counts as a delicacy, and which foods are forbidden. McDonald's India serves no beef or pork — patrons order a Chicken Maharaja Mac instead.

Dietary systemWhat it means for product & menu
KashrutJewish law; kosher foods are “fit,” excluding some meats including pork. Rabbis supervise production.
HalalMuslim law; haraam (forbidden) foods include pork and alcohol.
Hindu dietary lawAvoid beef; stricter Hindus avoid all meat, some avoid dairy or eggs.

Americans increasingly choose fat-free options; most European consumers prefer full-fat versions of the same product (Aubrey, 2014).

Subjective Culture

The symbolic side: ideas, values, beliefs

Subjective culture consists of ideas worth passing to the next generation — language, and economic, educational, political, legal, philosophical, and religious systems, plus aesthetics and unstated assumptions.

These beliefs shape how people behave as consumers and how they respond to marketing stimuli. This is where “kitto katsu” and gift-giving rituals live.

Elements of subjective culture (Table 2.3)

Language
Official languages and local dialects
Values
Strongly held concepts present in a group
Norms
Social rules that reflect cultural values
Folkways & mores
Customs that dictate how people act socially
Roles & schemas
The “parts” and “scripts” members play in daily life
Origins of Culture · History

Traditions form slowly, then resist change

Language, dialect, and slang have roots centuries deep and evolve with the nation. French in Quebec differs from French in France — so Disney launched La chaîne Disney for Canadian Francophones, and KFC becomes Poulet Frit Kentucky by law.

Customs and rituals tie closely to religion and social institutions: lunar new-year traditions in East Asia, Ramadan where Islam prevails, even secular festivals like Spain's La Tomatina.

Cultural characteristics (Table 2.4)

  • Language, dialects, slang
  • Customs and rituals
  • Religions
  • Social institutions and practices
  • Political sphere
  • Common attitudes and beliefs
  • Aesthetics (concepts about beauty)
  • Views toward education
Origins of Culture · Geography

Land, climate, and contact shape custom

Topography

Mountains separate people, producing distinct languages — Switzerland's isolation yields four official tongues.

Population density

~half the world lives within 200 km of water. Density creates target markets for stores and services.

Climate

Cold climates centre life on hunting and warmth; hot climates favour outdoor life and siestas.

Access to others

Trade routes like the Silk Road spread ideas — Buddhism reached East Asia from India along it.

Surfing began in Polynesian culture, made possible by tropical islands surrounded by water, then spread to California in the 1930s. Geography seeds the customs a brand must later respect.

Culture & Values · Project GLOBE

A modern answer to Hofstede's critics

Project GLOBE is an international effort to update and extend cultural measurement. Like Hofstede, it builds awareness of differences across marketing strategy.

Power distance

Comfort with power and status differences.

Uncertainty avoidance

Reliance on norms & rules to reduce unpredictability.

Humane orientation

Rewarding fairness, altruism, and generosity.

Institutional & in-group collectivism

Collective resource distribution; pride & loyalty to groups and families.

Assertiveness & gender egalitarianism

Dominant behaviour; effort to limit gender inequality.

Future & performance orientation

Delaying gratification; rewarding excellence.

Applying Cultural Values to Marketing

From scores to segmentation — carefully

  • Cultural-dimension scores give a broad conceptualization of a country's values.
  • They guide international market segmentation and how to reach target markets.
  • Over 600 articles link Hofstede's values to behaviour — best predicting emotional responses, then attitudes, perceptions, behaviours.

Use with caution

Frameworks don't predict behaviour straightforwardly. Treat dimensions as a continuum, not extremes — no culture is purely individualistic or collectivistic.

Watch for cultural paradoxes: consumers from individualistic cultures may still prefer collectivistic appeals.

Time Orientation · Task Scheduling

Monochronic vs. polychronic time

Monochronic

Time is a line cut into blocks. One task at a time; interruptions cause chaos; schedules and punctuality matter. Most Western countries.

Polychronic

Time is flexible; many tasks run at once; schedules bend when a task needs more time.

These orientations matter for B2B buyer–seller relationships, scheduling deliveries, and timing promotional campaigns (Usunier & Lee, 2009).

Time Orientation · Temporal Focus

Past, present, and future cultures

Past-oriented

Value tradition, history, elders. Many European & Asian countries. Loans less popular — elders help fund homes & education.

Present-oriented

Time is cyclical; the future can't be controlled. Mexico. Consumers want to enjoy now — "buy now, pay later" works.

Future-oriented

Plan, save, delay gratification. USA. Retirement planning and long-term framing resonate.

A study of credit-purchase appeals across the USA, China, and Mexico found each market needs a different message: future benefits in the US, away-from-tradition themes in China, present benefits in Mexico (Spears et al., 2000).

Three Cultures World

Honor, achievement, and joy

From the World Values Survey: a view of the world as three main cultural groups, each rewarding a different marketing approach (Basanez, 2016).

Honor

Religion, tradition, hierarchy, strong authority. Group over individual. Driver: the state.

→ Advertise products that enhance social status.

Achievement

Egalitarianism, individualism, efficiency, productivity; secular. Driver: the market.

→ Hard sell, performance & effectiveness appeals.

Joy

Well-being, social responsibility, quality of life; private religion. Driver: the family.

→ Soft sell: fun, happiness, relaxation.

Global & Local Cultural Influences

Hybridization: homogeneity and difference at once

Media, music, and technology spread fast — Netflix reaches 190 countries. Local and global forces mix into new forms: hybridization.

Some embrace global influence; others resist it. The Slow Food movement (Italy) pushes back, defending regional food traditions against global brands.

Two reactions to global culture

  • Reject — oppose global-brand values (Slow Food).
  • Embrace — buy global brands as a marker of quality and shared global identity.

For many consumers, global brands (Table 2.12: Apple, Google, Coca-Cola…) form part of their social identity.

Consumer Culture

When consumption becomes meaning

Consumer culture

Consumption & possessions become the primary source of meaning. Rooted in department stores and world's fairs; now dominant in Western societies. Marketing principles spread to education, health care, even politics.

Global consumer culture

A globally recognised set of consumption behaviours, values, and brands. Critics argue global brands distort competition and create unrealistic aspirations (Benady, 2008).

The tension between global consumer culture and local resistance is exactly the terrain KitKat navigates — a global brand that earns its place by respecting local meaning.

Cultural Distance & Psychic Distance

How far apart are two markets?

Cultural distance

The degree to which values, norms, and beliefs differ between two countries — measured via economic development, education, language, and cultural dimensions. Outside the firm's control.

Psychic distance

An individual's subjective perception of the gap between home and foreign market. Shapes which markets and products managers favour — and can be addressed by selecting staff with low psychic distance.

Managers with more positive perceptions of a foreign market are likelier to succeed there. A good match raises the firm's probability of success (Sousa & Bradley, 2006).

Learning Objective #3

Culture & Behaviors

From greeting a stranger to closing a deal — culture governs the behaviours that make or break business relationships. The chapter sorts them into three buckets.
Behaviours · Three Categories

Imperatives, electives, and exclusives

Imperatives

Customs you must meet or must avoid for success. Building friendship before business (guanxi, ningen kankei). In Japan, prolonged eye contact offends; in Arab/Latin cultures, weak eye contact reads as evasive.

Electives

Customs you may, but need not, follow. A symbolic bow, accepting a business card with two hands, greeting in the local language. Optional, but they build rapport.

Exclusives

Customs reserved for locals — outsiders are barred. Religious ceremonies; a males-only mosque at the Taj Mahal; Parliament access limited for foreign visitors.

Subcultures & Countercultures

Worlds within the dominant culture

Subcultures

Distinct values & behaviours that still fit the dominant culture — ethnic groups, religious subgroups. Microcultures are smaller groups within them.

Marketing angle: Kingfisher Beer markets to Indian immigrants and locals in the UK — one of its ten fastest-growing beer brands there.

Countercultures

Values that set members in opposition to the dominant culture — challenging its core values, or simply choosing different lifestyles (vegans, the Amish avoiding advanced technology).

Cultural Change

Even traditional cultures shift

Cultures change slowly, but they change. Social media reshapes worldviews; Saudi Arabia's move to allow women to drive opened a fresh market for car sellers.

Two ways a marketing team can respond:

  • Cultural congruence — design for the current culture, adjust only as it shifts.
  • Promote change — riskier; can offend society or government.

Pizza Hut in China

Consumers once tried eating pizza with chopsticks. Pizza Hut made its menu congruent with local taste — adding non-pizza dishes, wine, local fish, flower-petal flavours, even a duck-topped pizza.

Culture & Purchasing · Aesthetics & Religion

Beauty and belief drive purchases

Aesthetics

Concepts of beauty shape cosmetics, clothing, jewellery, models, and design. Many Japanese consumers idealise thinness (the seaweed-based kanten diet); many African cultures view a heavier body as healthier and more attractive.

Religion

One dominant religion simplifies adaptation; multiple religions complicate it. Chicken Cottage grew to 140 UK outlets with halal food that also appeals to non-Muslims. Unilever pulled a Lux billboard within 24 hours after a rabbi objected.

Ethics, Social Responsibility & Culture

Relativism vs. absolutism

Ethical relativism

Ethical values are relative to a culture's norms — no universal standard. Nepotism is acceptable in many collectivist cultures, frowned upon or illegal in many Western ones.

Ethical absolutism

Some fundamental values cross all cultures and companies must uphold them — the basis of international codes of conduct.

Ethics reside in individuals, but firms should set clear rules — resolving dilemmas with the best interests of consumers and the environment in mind.

Case · Background

From a York factory to a global wafer

KitKat is a chocolate-covered wafer biscuit produced globally by Nestlé. The first bar — “Chocolate Crisp” — was sold by Rowntree's of York in 1935; the shape has barely changed since. The name traces to a 17th-century literary club that met at pastry chef Christopher Catling's pie shop.

Sweets are material culture: which sweets people prefer, and how they eat them, varies by region — the UK tea-time biscuit, the US "biscuit" that is really a scone.

Internationalization timeline

  • 1950s — Anglo-Saxon markets: Australia, Canada, NZ, South Africa
  • 1970s — Germany; US license to Hershey; Japan (1973)
  • 1980s — Japanese manufacturing facility opens
  • Today — made in Brazil, Mexico, UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, Russia, Japan, UAE, Bulgaria & more
Case · The Accident

“Kitto katsu” — you will surely win

KitKat sounds like the Japanese phrase “kitto katsu” — “you will surely win.” Students began buying the bars as good-luck charms for university entrance exams.

Nestlé noticed and operationalised the accident: in 2009 it partnered with Japan Post to launch the postable KitKat. Today about half of Japan's ~600,000 annual exam-sitting students receive one for luck.

1973first KitKat on sale in Japan
~50%of exam students gifted one yearly
5Mbars consumed in Japan every day
300+varieties, wasabi to melon

Crucially, “Have a Break, Have a KitKat” does not carry the same meaning in Japan — so Nestlé built the brand differently there. Accidents reward the prepared.

Case Q2 · Cultural Rituals

Attach the bar to rituals the culture already owns

Q2: Which cultural traditions and practices could Nestlé relate KitKat to? In Poland it joins śmigus Easter gifts for godchildren; in the UK it joins the office tea break. The bar slots into existing customs.

Gift & celebration

Easter śmigus baskets, exam good-luck gifts, festive gifting — a small, shareable token of goodwill.

Daily consumption

The British tea break, the coffee pause — “Have a Break” fits a habit the consumer already keeps.

Social & sharing

Snapping fingers to share among friends — the product's form supports the occasion.

Nestlé must also weigh each market's political & regulatory context — taxation, labelling, import/export duties, stability — and run a SWOT to choose where each tradition-led play can profitably deploy.

Case Q3 · The Flavour Explosion

Why 300+ flavours in Japan — and not elsewhere?

  • Short product life cycles. Japan's confectionery market moves fast; constant novelty keeps the brand talked-about.
  • Fierce convenience-store competition. A crowded shelf rewards a rotating, limited-edition line-up.
  • Scarcity & rarity of value. Seasonal editions become collectible, generating their own hype and social-media publicity.
  • Gift & omiyage culture. Local-flavour souvenirs fit Japan's regional gifting.

Why not everywhere?

Many markets have more open shelf space to present the product simply, and palates favour a stable core range. Cultural values differ by country, so Nestlé develops and markets products by local demand and geography — innovating heavily where the market rewards it, standardising where it doesn't.

Critical Topic · Standardization vs. Adaptation

Think globally, act locally

Standardize — “think globally”

  • One consistent name, logo, and red livery worldwide
  • The tagline “Have a Break, Have a KitKat” everywhere
  • A simple, meaningful brand that differentiates and scales cheaply

Adapt — “act locally”

  • 200+ flavours & editions, most made in Japan
  • Lucky-charm “kitto katsu” positioning
  • Postable gift formats tuned to local rituals
KitKat keeps a strong, consistent brand image and adapts to unique local needs. Innovation revitalises the brand, creates products worth talking about, and generates its own hype — especially on social media. Synthesised from instructor feedback on the Group 5 submission

Takeaways

What Chapter 2 and KitKat teach

Culture comes first

Material and subjective culture, origins, values, time orientation, and the behaviour map should be examined before any marketing decision.

Standardize the brand, adapt the mix

A consistent global identity plus locally tuned flavour, meaning, and channel. “Think globally, act locally” is the strategic spine.

Accidents reward the prepared

“Kitto katsu” was luck — Nestlé read the culture, then amplified and operationalised it into a durable position.

Match innovation to the market

Heavy flavour innovation pays where competition and cycles demand it (Japan); a stable core works where they don't.
